Why carbon markets need a practical safety valve

Balancing decarbonisation ambition with the stability industry needs to keep investing through the energy transition.

Carbon markets only work if the people they are meant to steer actually participate. And participation falls away the moment the price of a tonne of CO₂ becomes a source of unbudgetable risk. The lesson from the last decade is simple: ambition without a safety valve produces volatility, and volatility produces abandoned programmes.

Volatility is the enemy of long-horizon investment

Decarbonisation is a thirty-year problem. Few boards will commit capital against a compliance cost that can triple or collapse inside a single budget cycle. When the price signal whipsaws, the rational response is to wait — and waiting is exactly what the transition cannot afford.

A carbon price that nobody can plan around prices the transition out of existence.

What a safety valve actually looks like

The mechanism is well understood from other commodities: a credible ceiling (a cost-containment reserve or allowance buffer) that releases supply if prices spike, paired with a floor that preserves incentive when prices sag. It is not a loophole. It is what makes the market governable.

  • A ceiling caps the worst-case compliance shock, keeping industry at the table.
  • A floor protects the investment case for abatement when demand is soft.
  • A predictable band lets treasuries finance real, physical reductions.

Our position

We trade carbon because we believe liquid, workable markets accelerate the transition — but only if they are built to survive a downturn. We support design that prioritises durability over headline stringency, and we engage with the schemes we operate in to push for exactly that.

The alternative — markets that lurch from boom to bust — does not reduce a single tonne in the long run. It just pushes the hard decisions further into the future.
