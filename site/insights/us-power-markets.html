What matters most in US power markets right now

Rising electricity demand is reshaping how reliable supply is developed, fuelled and delivered — and where the real opportunities lie.

For two decades, US power was a story of flat demand and falling prices. That era is over. Data-centre load, electrified transport and reshoring have pushed forecasts the other way, and the market is now scrambling to rebuild the reliability it spent years trimming out of the system.

The demand step-change is real, not a forecast artefact

The shift is visible in interconnection queues that now stretch years into the future, and in capacity prices that have moved from a rounding error to a line item. When load grows this fast, the binding constraint stops being generation and becomes delivery — transmission, siting and the mundane plumbing of getting electrons to where they are needed.

The opportunity is no longer “who can build the cheapest megawatt” — it is “who can deliver a dependable megawatt where and when the grid needs it.”

Firm capacity is the scarce asset

Intermittent renewable build-out has been spectacular, but it has not retired the need for dispatchable, firm capacity. Gas-fired generation — and increasingly storage paired with gas — is what keeps the lights on when the wind drops and the data centre keeps drawing. That reframes gas from a transition liability into transition infrastructure.

  • Locational scarcity is now the dominant price signal in several hubs.
  • Merchant risk is back, which means hedging and structured offtake matter again.
  • Behind-the-meter deals are quietly reshaping who actually consumes power.

Where we see the edge

We are positioned where physical capability meets market structure: flexible generation, storage co-location, and the trading relationships to move power across regions as prices diverge. The desks that win this cycle will be the ones that own optionality — the ability to produce, store or withhold — rather than a single static position.

None of this is a bet on any one technology. It is a bet on a grid that needs to be managed more actively than at any point in a generation, and on the operators disciplined enough to do it well.
